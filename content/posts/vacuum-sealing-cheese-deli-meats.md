# How to Vacuum Seal Cheese, Deli Meats & Salami — Stop Mould, Extend Fridge Life 3–5x

You bring home a block of parmesan or a few slices of prosciutto from the deli, wrap them up in their paper, and a week later open the fridge to find blue-green mould on the cut face of the cheese, or the ham slices slimy around the edges and smelling off. Out it goes.

Cheese and deli meats are some of the most frequently wasted foods in Australian fridges. They're also some of the easiest to extend the life of — if you store them correctly. Vacuum sealing works exceptionally well for cheese and cured meats, but there are a few tricks, especially with soft cheese and sliced deli meat.

## Why cheese and deli meats go bad so fast

Two culprits:

1. **Oxygen.** Mould and aerobic bacteria need oxygen to grow. Original deli paper and supermarket plastic wrap aren't airtight — air circulates, mould spores land on the cut surface and start colonies in days.
2. **Condensation cycles.** As the fridge cycles on and off, condensation forms inside the packaging. Slimy deli meat is bacteria growing in trapped moisture; cheese dries and cracks as surface moisture evaporates, then cracks grow mould.

Vacuum sealing removes oxygen and holds moisture stable at the food surface. It doesn't make food last forever, but it typically extends usable fridge life **3–5x longer** than original packaging.

## How much extra life does it buy you?

Typical fridge life at 2–4°C:

| Food | Original / opened wrap | Cling wrap / zip-lock | Vacuum sealed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hard cheese (parmesan, pecorino) | 3–4 weeks | 4–6 weeks | 4–6 months |
| Firm cheese (cheddar, gouda) | 2–3 weeks | 3–4 weeks | 3–4 months |
| Blue cheese | 2–3 weeks | 2–3 weeks | 3–4 weeks |
| Sliced deli ham / turkey | 3–5 days | 5–7 days | 2–3 weeks |
| Prosciutto / bresaola (whole) | 2–3 weeks | 2–3 weeks | 2–3 months |
| Hard salami (whole piece) | 3–4 weeks | 4–6 weeks | 4–6 months |
| Smoked salmon | 3–5 days | 5–7 days | 2–3 weeks |

The biggest wins are on hard cheese, hard salami and cured meats — exactly the expensive stuff you don't want to throw out.

## The soft-cheese rule

**Don't vacuum seal soft surface-ripened cheeses (brie, camembert, washed rind) for more than a couple of weeks.** Those cheeses are alive — the white rind is a living culture that needs to breathe. Stuck in full vacuum, the rind can suffocate and the cheese can develop off-flavours or unwanted anaerobic bacteria. A week or two while you finish a wheel is fine; longer term, stick to cheese paper in a loosely closed container. Blue cheese seals fine, but pull only a mild vacuum so you don't squeeze the veins into mush.

## Step-by-step: sealing a block of cheese

Hard and firm cheeses (cheddar, parmesan, gouda, manchego) are the easiest and most rewarding.

1. **Start clean and dry.** Pat the cut face lightly with paper towel if damp.
2. **Portion into 200–300g pieces** (a week or two of use). You want to open one portion at a time, not repeatedly break seal on the same block. For long-term storage, wrap in a small piece of baking paper first to prevent sticking.
3. **Bag it** with a few centimetres of room on all sides so the zip closes cleanly.
4. **Zip end to end.** A half-zipped bag is an unsealed bag. A press-to-seal zip where you drag a finger leaves small gaps you can't see — the #1 cause of vacuum loss. A zip-slider that rides the track forces it closed end to end. Even better is a slider permanently attached to the bag. FreshLock bags use an attached apple-green zip-slider that clicks onto the track so it can't get lost in a drawer; run it across once and you know the seal is closed.
5. **Pull a firm vacuum** until the bag tightens around the cheese and the block shows through the plastic. For semi-soft or blue cheese, stop at gentle tightness so you don't squish it.
6. **Label and date.** Six-month-old parmesan looks a lot like last week's cheddar in the back of the fridge.
7. **Fridge or freeze.** Hard cheese sits in the cheese drawer for 3–4 months sealed. Longer than that, freeze it — it thaws a little crumbly but is perfectly fine for pasta, sauces and grating.

If you're rescuing a half-block already in the fridge, trim any very dry edges first, then seal. Don't seal cheese with visible mould — trim 1cm around any spot and use it today.

## Step-by-step: sliced deli meats (ham, turkey, roast beef)

Sliced deli meat is trickier because slices stick when compressed and high surface area grows bacteria fast if air remains.

1. **Stack small portions** of 4–6 slices (a few days' worth). Separate with small squares of baking paper if you want to peel them off individually.
2. **Pat dry** with paper towel — deli slices are often damp from brine, and pooled liquid at the bottom of the bag spoils fastest.
3. **Lay flat in the bag** in a thin even layer for even vacuum pull and faster freezing/thawing.
4. **Zip, then pull a gentle vacuum.** Don't crush — pull just until the bag tightens around the stack. Full vacuum welds slices into a brick.
5. **Store on a fridge shelf** (not the door, which cycles in temperature).
6. **Freeze for long-term.** Sealed portions freeze well for 2–3 months; thaw in the fridge in a few hours.

## Whole cured meats and hard salami

Whole pieces of prosciutto, bresaola, saucisson and hard salami are the ultimate vacuum seal candidates — expensive, dry, low-moisture, and they don't crush under vacuum. Seal as a whole piece or chunk (no paper needed), pull full vacuum tight around the sausage, and store in the fridge (or cool dark pantry for shelf-stable salami — check the label). Once you open a chunk, slice what you need and re-seal. Hard salami sealed this way lasts 6 months in the fridge without mould or drying.

## Common mistakes to avoid

- **Full vacuum on soft cheese or thin-sliced meat** — you end up with brie paste or a turkey brick. Gentle vacuum is enough.
- **Sealing wet brine or pooled meat juice** — pat dry first.
- **Skipping the slider** — a half-zipped bag is an unsealed bag.
- **Sealing mouldy cheese to "save it."** Mould roots go deeper than the visible spot.
- **Freezing soft cheese** — brie, camembert, feta and fresh mozzarella turn grainy and weep water when thawed.
