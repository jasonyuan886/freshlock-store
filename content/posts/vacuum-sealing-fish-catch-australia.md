## Why Fish Goes Bad Faster Than Other Meat

Fish spoils faster and gets freezer burn more easily than red meat. Three reasons:

1. **Omega-3 oils oxidise fast** when exposed to oxygen — that's the "fishy" cardboard taste on old fillets.
2. **Delicate muscle structure.** Short fibres and little connective tissue mean ice crystals rupture cells faster during freezing, leading to mushy texture.
3. **Enzymes keep working** even in the fridge; only freezing stops them, and air exposure speeds the process.

Freezer burn is air exposure, plain and simple — moisture sublimates straight from ice to vapour, leaving dry, discoloured patches. Vacuum sealing removes almost all the air; an airtight zipper keeps it out. Done properly, a vacuum-sealed fillet at -18°C doesn't develop freezer burn and tastes fresh months later. ### Boat-side workflow

1. **Prep at home.** Pack 20–30 embossed bags in mixed sizes, filleting knife, board, paper towel, and the sealer in a dry bag. Charge fully the night before.
2. **Kill and bleed immediately** — non-negotiable for taste and for less mess in bags.
3. **Fillet into bags**, one or two fillets per bag.
4. **Pat dry** with paper towel to remove surface blood and slime.
5. **Zip** with the attached apple-green slider.
6. **Seal** in short bursts, 15–20 seconds total.
7. **Drop straight into ice slurry.** Back home, bags go straight to freezer — no repackaging.

## Fish Blood vs. Your Sealer: The Overflow Problem

Fish is wet — even bled and patted dry, fillets leak juice, oily fish leak a lot, and prawns hold water. That moisture is what makes fresh fish taste great, but it can wreck a sealer that isn't built for it. Watch pinkish liquid get sucked into the nozzle of a cheap handheld, and it either dies immediately or slowly rusts and smells of fish forever. It's the same problem we cover in our [liquids and marinades guide](/blog/vacuum-seal-liquids-sauces-marinades-without-motor-damage), but it's extra frustrating on a boat with a day's catch and no backup.

That's why the FreshLock Pro uses a **purely mechanical auto-overflow design**. At the base, a transparent collection cup catches liquid pulled from the bag. If the cup fills, excess drains through a physical overflow channel, away from the motor — no electronics, no sensors, nothing to fail. Between bags, glance at the cup; if it has blood or slime water, pop it off, empty over the side, click back on.

And because there are no electronics in the fluid path, you can actually clean it when you get home — hold the base in warm soapy water, run the sealer for a few seconds to flush the air channel, repeat with clean water, air-dry. No trapped fish smell, no mould in hidden airways (a common complaint with sensor-equipped sealers you can't wash). Note: the motor/battery/USB-C housing stays dry; only the cup and air channel area is rinsable, because there's nothing electronic there.

When sealing fish, pulse the suction rather than running it continuously. Tight is enough — crush-the-fillet tight is unnecessary and increases liquid risk.

## How Long Does Vacuum-Sealed Fish Last?

Exact shelf life depends on freezer temperature (-18°C is ideal) and how the fish was handled, but as a rough Australian-conditions guide:

| Species | Fridge (vacuum-sealed, <4°C) | Freezer (vacuum-sealed, -18°C) |
|---|---|---|
| Snapper, Barramundi, Coral Trout | 3–4 days | 6–8 months |
| Prawns (green, raw) | 2–3 days | 4–6 months |
| Australian Salmon, Tuna | 2–3 days | 3–4 months |
| Mackerel | 2 days | 2–3 months |
| Flathead, Whiting, Bream | 3 days | 6 months |
| Squid / Calamari | 2–3 days | 4–6 months |

That's roughly triple the freezer life of cling wrap or standard freezer bags, where 4–8 weeks is the norm before freezer burn sets in.

One thing vacuum sealing does *not* do: replace ice. You still need to keep fish cold — an ice slurry (seawater + ice = roughly -1°C) is gold standard on the boat. Vacuum sealing keeps meltwater out, protects fillets, and stops fishy smells from contaminating everything else in the esky.

## Tips for Boat Trips and 4WD Camping

- **Charge the night before** (100+ seals per charge). For week-long trips, bring a 20,000mAh power bank or charge off the car USB — USB-C is everywhere now.
- **Keep the sealer in a dry bag.** Saltwater and electronics don't mix.
- **Pre-portion bags at home** and label ("snapper", "prawns", "bait") for fast bagging on the water.
- **Bag bait too.** Vacuum-sealed pilchards and squid don't leak goo over the tackle box; unused portions go back in the freezer.
- **Freeze bags flat** on a tray for 24 hours before stacking — faster freeze, faster thaw, less space.

## Common Mistakes & FAQ

**Whole fish or fillets?** Fillets are more practical — flatter, freeze and thaw faster, no wasted space. You can seal whole gutted fish if you have large enough bags.

**Do I need to freeze fish before sealing?** No. Freeze-first is for soups and pure liquids (see our [liquids guide](/blog/vacuum-seal-liquids-sauces-marinades-without-motor-damage)). Seal fresh with pulse suction and drop into slurry.

**Bones piercing bags?** Snip sharp spines with pliers, wrap bony ends in a small piece of butcher paper, or double-bag. A missed pinhole is the #1 reason bags "unseal" in the freezer.

**How to thaw?** Best: leave sealed on a plate in the fridge overnight. Fast: submerge the sealed bag in cold water 30–60 minutes. Don't refreeze fully thawed fish — cook within a day. [Sous vide](/blog/sous-vide-vacuum-sealing-guide) straight from frozen works great.

**Reusing fish bags?** Not recommended — raw fish bacteria makes cross-contamination a risk. Treat as single-use.

**Cleaning after a fishy day?** Empty the cup each session. Back home, rinse the cup and air channel through with warm soapy water (hold base in a bowl, run sealer 5–10 seconds, repeat with clean water), then air-dry with the cup off. Don't submerge the whole sealer — the top housing isn't designed for it. Never put any part in the dishwasher.
