# How to Prevent Freezer Burn on Meat, Fish & Bread — The Vacuum Sealing Fix That Actually Works

You reach into the freezer for that pack of steaks you bought on special, and the corners are greyish-brown, dry, crumbly, dotted with ice crystals. It's not unsafe to eat, but when you cook it you get tough, tasteless, watery patches — and half of it ends up in the bin.

That's freezer burn. Australian households throw out hundreds of dollars of frozen food every year because of it: meat, fish, bread, berries, leftovers, all slowly dehydrating even when they're "sealed." This guide explains what freezer burn is, why the cheap fixes don't work, and how to prevent it reliably with vacuum sealing.

## What Is Freezer Burn?

Freezer burn isn't bacteria and it isn't spoilage. It's dehydration caused by sublimation — water molecules in frozen food turn straight from ice into water vapour, escape into the air inside the packaging, and refreeze as frost on the bag or freezer wall.

Two things happen as water leaves the food surface:

1. **Dry, discoloured patches.** Meat turns grey-brown and fish develops white cottony spots because oxygen reacts with pigments once the protective moisture layer is gone.
2. **Tough, flavourless texture.** The cells that lost water don't rehydrate evenly when cooked, leaving spongey or leathery patches instead of juicy bite.

The root cause is always the same: **air inside the packaging**. No air = nowhere for water vapour to escape into = no freezer burn.

## Three Factors That Control How Fast It Shows Up

- **How much air is in the pack.** Loosely wrapped steak burns in 2–4 weeks; properly vacuum-sealed steak lasts 6–12 months.
- **Temperature fluctuation.** Every time the freezer warms up (door left open, auto-defrost cycle, warm groceries going in), surface ice melts slightly and refreezes elsewhere, speeding up sublimation.
- **The food itself.** Fatty foods oxidise faster, bread dries out within weeks, lean fish burns faster than red meat, and berries turn mushy when internal ice crystals pierce cell walls.

## The "Fixes" That Don't Really Work

If you've been double-wrapping meat in cling film or squeezing air out of zip-lock bags by hand, you already know the results are disappointing:

- **Cling film** is permeable to air over time. Good for a few days in the fridge, useless after a month in the freezer.
- **Supermarket zip-lock freezer bags** are thicker, but the zip isn't airtight and you can never squeeze all the air out by hand. That remaining pocket of air is where freezer burn starts.
- **Aluminium foil** blocks air better but tears easily and doesn't seal along edges.
- **Double-bagging** adds layers but doesn't solve the core problem: trapped air against the food.

All of these slow freezer burn down. None of them stop it. The only reliable prevention is removing the air entirely before freezing.

## How Vacuum Sealing Stops Freezer Burn

Vacuum sealing pulls air out of the bag before sealing, so there's nowhere for water vapour to sublimate into. The bag shrinks tight around the food, eliminating air pockets and pressing plastic directly against the surface — that tight contact stops both dehydration and oxidation.

Typical home-freezer shelf life at -18°C:

| Food | Loosely wrapped | Zip-lock bag | Vacuum sealed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Raw steak / mince | 2–4 weeks | 2–3 months | 6–12 months |
| Chicken pieces | 1–2 months | 3–4 months | 6–9 months |
| White fish (snapper, barra) | 3–4 weeks | 2–3 months | 6–8 months |
| Salmon / oily fish | 2–3 weeks | 1–2 months | 3–5 months |
| Bread (sliced/loaf) | 1–2 weeks | 1 month | 3–6 months |
| Berries | — | 6–8 months | 1–2 years |
| Cooked leftovers | 1–2 weeks | 1 month | 3–4 months |

Chest freezers at steady temperature give the longer end; frost-free uprights give the shorter end.

### Two mistakes that still cause burn with a sealer

1. **Zip track not fully closed.** If even a centimetre isn't sealed, air leaks back in over a week. A puffed-up bag in the freezer is the giveaway.
2. **Air pockets in crevices.** On irregular shapes (whole chicken, stacked patties), press the bag flat around the food before sealing.

## Step-by-Step: Freezing Meat Without Freezer Burn

About a minute per portion:

1. **Portion first.** Divide mince into 500g blocks, two steaks per bag, single-meal portions. You don't want to thaw a whole kilo for two people.
2. **Pat dry.** Surface moisture freezes into ice crystals and can be pulled toward the valve.
3. **Bag and zip.** Leave about 5cm headspace. Run the zip firmly end to end.
4. **Vacuum.** Attach the sealer and pull until the bag is tight and meat texture shows through the plastic. The FreshLock Pro pulls -60kPa of suction — enough for long freezer life.
5. **Label and date.** Masking tape and a Sharpie. First in, first out saves money.
6. **Freeze flat** on a tray for a few hours until solid, then stack vertically like books. Thaws faster, saves space.

## Can You Fix Already-Burned Meat?

Not really. Once surface moisture is gone you can't put it back. You can trim the worst grey patches and use the rest in slow-cooked stews or curries where sauce masks texture loss, but quality is already degraded. Prevention is the only real fix — five minutes of bagging on the day you bring meat home saves you from throwing out $20 steaks three months later.

## The Bottom Line

Freezer burn isn't inevitable — it's just air doing what air does. Remove the air before food goes into the freezer and you extend the life of meat 3–5x, fish 2–3x, and bread and berries dramatically.
